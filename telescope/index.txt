                <p>The **Primary Mirror** is the centerpiece of JWST. It’s a 6.5-meter segmented mirror made of 18 hexagonal segments. This mirror collects light from distant galaxies and stars, allowing the telescope to see deeper into space than ever before.</p>
                <p><strong>Importance:</strong> The mirror's size allows for the capture of faint infrared signals from the early universe, helping to study the formation of stars and planets.</p>

                <h2>Sunshield</h2>
                <p>The **Sunshield** consists of five layers of specially coated materials designed to protect the telescope's optics from the Sun’s heat. It ensures that the sensitive instruments stay cool at about -230°C.</p>
                <p><strong>Importance:</strong> This cooling is essential to infrared observations, as heat would otherwise interfere with the data collection.</p>
            </div>

                <h2>Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM)</h2>
                <p>The **ISIM** holds the four main scientific instruments that capture and analyze the light collected by the primary mirror. It is crucial for imaging and spectroscopy.</p>
                <p><strong>Importance:</strong> The module enables astronomers to conduct detailed studies of planets, stars, and galaxies in the infrared spectrum.</p>
            </div>
        </section>

                <h2>Secondary Mirror</h2>
                <p>The **Secondary Mirror** is smaller and reflects the light from the primary mirror into the telescope's instruments.</p>
                <p><strong>Importance:</strong> This precise reflection ensures that the data is correctly captured by the sensors for analysis.</p>
            </div>
        </section>

            <div class="text-box">
                <h2>Spacecraft Bus</h2>
                <p>The **Spacecraft Bus** houses the observatory's computing systems, power, communication equipment, and propulsion.</p>
                <p><strong>Importance:</strong> This subsystem controls the position of the telescope and powers all the onboard instruments, making sure the telescope functions correctly in space.</p>
